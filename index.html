PHY1400 · Conceptual Physics · School of Science & Engineering

Physics Accuracy in VR Simulations

A data-driven policy approach for integrating immersive technologies in Moroccan science curricula — addressing the Fidelity Gap between game engine physics and scientific reality under Digital Morocco 2030.

Adam Elhamaoui

Ministry of National Education
Ministry of Digital Transition
Kingdom of Morocco

Al Akhawayn University
Ifrane, Morocco · Spring 2026

13 February 2026

Abstract

Most educational VR applications are built on game engines optimised for visual throughput rather than physical accuracy, producing a Fidelity Gap between how realistic a simulation looks and how correct it actually is. Unchecked, this risks teaching students Aristotelian misconceptions, unsafe laboratory habits, and corrupted experimental data. This paper evaluates three regulatory pathways and recommends the Pedagogical Fidelity Framework (MSE-VR) — a tiered certification standard managed by the CNIPE that demands accuracy where it matters, permits helpful idealisation where it aids comprehension, and remains financially viable for all Moroccan schools.

GAP
The Problem §01

The Fidelity Gap: Three Technical
Failures of Game Engine Physics

Engines like Unity and Unreal maximise visual frame rate, not physical accuracy. Their computational shortcuts accumulate into three distinct categories of scientific failure with direct pedagogical consequences.

Technical Failure 01

Energy Leak — Integration Error

Standard Semi-Implicit Euler integration updates position from old velocity each frame. Accumulated rounding error in oscillating systems causes energy to grow spontaneously — a virtual pendulum swings progressively higher, violating Conservation of Energy without any external input.

Technical Failure 02

Tunneling Effect

High-velocity objects traverse so much distance per frame that collision detection misses the boundary. Projectiles, electrons, and fast-moving particles teleport through solid barriers — directly contradicting what students are meant to learn about material physics and boundary conditions.

Technical Failure 03

Optical Distortion & Fluid Fakes

VR lens pincushion distortion curves straight laser beams; chromatic aberration blurs measurement lines. Water rendering is a pure cosmetic shader — lacking diffraction, interference, or wave propagation — adding cognitive load with zero scientific content.

Pedagogical Risks §02
1

Negative Transfer

Students trained on weightless VR controllers mishandle real equipment — a 100 kg virtual mass is indistinguishable from 1 kg. Unsafe habits carry directly into physical laboratories.

2

Misconception Reinforcement

Default linear-damping algorithms decelerate all virtual objects. Students observe motion decay in a vacuum and conclude "motion requires force" — actively teaching the Aristotelian fallacy Newton disproved.

3

Cognitive Overload

Photorealistic textures and particle effects that lack scientific function consume working memory, diverting attention from the core physics learning objective.

Policy Options Evaluated §03
1

Laissez-Faire — Market-Driven

Unrestricted app-store adoption. Fast and inexpensive with zero vetting. High risk of gamified misconceptions and negative transfer across all school types.

2

Scientific Realism Mandate

Require NASA-grade accuracy (<1% error, full fluid dynamics). Eliminates errors but demands expensive PC-VR hardware inaccessible to rural schools.

3

Pedagogical Fidelity Framework

Tiered certification judging software by its learning objective — rigour for data collection, helpful idealisation for concepts. Practical and cost-viable nationally.

Recommended
The Moroccan Standard for Educational VR (MSE-VR) — Three-Tier Framework §04
Tier I · Concept Builder

Abstract Validation

Goal: Teaching Laws & Principles

Simplifications such as zero friction or idealised point-masses are permitted to isolate variables. The underlying mathematics must remain internally consistent — auto-damping and engine shortcuts must be disabled so objects behave predictably, even if not realistically.

Standard: Consistent Idealisation
Tier II · Virtual Lab

Empirical Validation

Goal: Data Collection & Measurement

Strict predictive accuracy required. Simulations must use high-precision Runge-Kutta RK4 integrators and Continuous Collision Detection (CCD). Generated experimental data must match real-world results within a 5% margin of error. No tunneling permitted.

Standard: ±5% Error / RK4 + CCD Mandatory
Tier III · Immersive Experience

Phenomenological Validation

Goal: Field Simulations & Exploration

Visual immersion and presence take precedence — experiences like walking on Mars prioritise the phenomenological sensation. Scientific accuracy is secondary, but every simulation must pass a structured Negative Transfer safety audit before deployment.

Standard: Plausibility + Safety Audit
Developer Implementation Guidelines §05
Guideline 01

Disable Default Linear Damping

Remove auto-drag from vacuum simulations to prevent Aristotelian artifacts — objects must not decelerate without explicit applied forces.

Guideline 02

Adopt RK4 Integration

Replace Euler loops with Runge-Kutta 4 for all oscillating and orbital systems to conserve energy correctly across simulation frames.

Guideline 03

Enable Continuous Collision Detection

CCD is mandatory for all Tier II projectile and high-velocity particle experiments to eliminate tunneling through solid boundaries.

Guideline 04

Visual Cues over Photorealism (Tier I)

Use schematic textures, labelled force vectors, and stripped-back palettes in conceptual apps to reduce cognitive load.

Guideline 05

Lens Distortion Correction

Full pincushion and chromatic aberration correction required wherever students measure vectors, angles, or distances in VR experiments.

Guideline 06

Haptic Safety Calibration (Tier III)

Mass representation must include resistance cues or visual mass labels so students do not develop unsafe handling assumptions for heavy equipment.

Evaluation & Monitoring §06

Random sampling of Tier II apps ensures generated data matches textbook physics within the 5% threshold. Non-compliant apps are de-certified by CNIPE.

Periodic surveys of physics instructors capture reports of student "video-game habits" or persistent conceptual misconceptions traceable to specific VR tools.

Review ensures no dangerous mass-handling habits are instilled, and confirms alignment with Programme GENIE for equitable nationwide deployment.

Conclusion

Protecting Scientific Truth Without Breaking the Budget

The Fidelity Gap is not merely a technical quirk — it is an educational hazard. Adopting the Pedagogical Fidelity Framework equips Morocco's Ministry with a practical, evidence-based instrument that protects scientific truth, prevents real-world harm from negative transfer, and delivers the cost-sustainability required for equitable rollout under Digital Morocco 2030. We do not need VR to be a perfect copy of reality; we need it to be a perfect tool for the learning objective.

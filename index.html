Executive Abstract

Most educational VR applications are built on game engines optimised for visual throughput rather than physical accuracy, producing a Fidelity Gap between how realistic a simulation looks and how correct it actually is. Unchecked, this risks teaching students Aristotelian misconceptions, unsafe laboratory habits, and corrupted experimental data. This paper recommends the Pedagogical Fidelity Framework (MSE-VR) — a tiered certification standard that demands accuracy where it matters and remains financially viable for all Moroccan schools.

The Core Issue

The Fidelity Gap: Three Technical Failures

Engines like Unity and Unreal maximise visual frame rate, not physical accuracy. Their computational shortcuts accumulate into three distinct categories of scientific failure with direct pedagogical consequences.

Failure 01

Energy Leak

Standard integration updates position from old velocity each frame. Accumulated rounding error causes energy to grow spontaneously — a virtual pendulum swings progressively higher, violating Conservation of Energy.

Failure 02

Tunneling Effect

High-velocity objects traverse so much distance per frame that collision detection misses the boundary. Projectiles and electrons teleport through solid barriers, contradicting material physics.

Failure 03

Optical Distortion

VR lens pincushion distortion curves straight laser beams. Fluid rendering is a pure cosmetic shader lacking diffraction or wave propagation, adding cognitive load with zero scientific content.

Implications

Pedagogical Risks

1

Negative Transfer

Students trained on weightless VR controllers mishandle real equipment — a 100 kg virtual mass feels identical to 1 kg, carrying unsafe habits to physical labs.

2

Misconception Reinforcement

Default drag algorithms decelerate objects even in virtual vacuums. Students conclude "motion requires force" — actively teaching the Aristotelian fallacy.

Strategy

Policy Options

01

Market-Driven Laissez-Faire

Unrestricted app-store adoption. Fast but high risk of negative transfer.

02

Scientific Realism Mandate

Demands NASA-grade accuracy, requiring inaccessible PC-VR hardware.

03

Pedagogical Fidelity Framework

Tiered certification judging software by learning objective. Practical and viable.

Recommended
We do not need VR to be a perfect replica of reality — we need it to be a perfect replica of the learning objective.
The Solution

The MSE-VR Framework

A three-tier regulatory standard for Moroccan Educational VR.

Tier I

Abstract Validation

Goal: Concept Building

Simplifications (zero friction, point-masses) are permitted to isolate variables. Mathematics must remain internally consistent; engine shortcuts must be disabled so objects behave predictably.

Standard: Consistent Idealisation
Tier II

Empirical Validation

Goal: Data Collection

Strict predictive accuracy. Simulations must use RK4 integrators and Continuous Collision Detection. Data must match real-world results within a 5% margin. No tunneling permitted.

Standard: ±5% Error Margin
Tier III

Phenomenological

Goal: Field Immersion

Visual immersion takes precedence (e.g., walking on Mars). Scientific accuracy is secondary, but every simulation must pass a structured safety audit before deployment.

Standard: Safety Audit Check
Execution

Dev Guidelines

Rule 01

Disable Default Linear Damping

Remove auto-drag from vacuum simulations to prevent Aristotelian artifacts.

Rule 02

Adopt RK4 Integration

Replace Euler loops with Runge-Kutta 4 for energy conservation.

Rule 03

Visual Cues over Photorealism

Use schematic textures and labelled vectors to reduce cognitive load.

Governance

Monitoring

Accuracy Sampling

Random checks of Tier II apps ensure data matches textbooks within 5%.

Feedback Loops

Surveys capture reports of student "video-game habits" traceable to VR tools.

National Alignment

Ensures equitable nationwide deployment aligning with Programme GENIE.
